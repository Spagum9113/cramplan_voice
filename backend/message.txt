Unlike classical mechanics, quantum mechanics introduces the concept of quantization, where certain properties, like energy, can only take discrete values. At the core of quantum physics are the wave-particle duality and the uncertainty principle. The former suggests that particles such as electrons exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties. The latter, formalized by Werner Heisenberg, states that certain pairs of physical properties, like position and momentum, cannot be precisely measured simultaneously. Quantum mechanics has led to the development of quantum fields, quantum entanglement, and superposition, directly impacting modern technologies such as semiconductors, lasers, and quantum computing. ContentText(topic_title='Classical Mechanics', content_text='Classical mechanics is the branch of physics that deals with the motion of bodies under the influence of forces. It is grounded in the works of Sir Isaac Newton, who formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation. These laws explain how objects move and interact, providing predictions that were applied for centuries to various fields, from engineering to astronomy. Classical mechanics primarily deals with macroscopic objects and considers time and space as absolute. It is deterministic, meaning given the initial conditions of a system, its future behavior can be precisely predicted. Although highly successful and intuitive, classical mechanics eventually required modification to accommodate the peculiarities of atomic and subatomic scales, giving rise to quantum mechanics. Nonetheless, it remains valid for a broad range of everyday phenomena, from the trajectory of a baseball to the orbits of planets, and serves as the foundation for much of physical science and engineering practice.'), 

ContentText(topic_title='Thermodynamics', content_text='Thermodynamics is the branch of physics focused on the principles governing energy and heat transfer. It studies the effects of changes in temperature, pressure, and volume on physical systems, resulting in macroscopic quantities like internal energy, entropy, and free energy. The foundational concepts of thermodynamics are encapsulated in its four laws. The first law, or the law of energy conservation, states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. The second law introduces the concept of entropy, suggesting that energy systems tend to progress towards disorder, emphasizing the irreversibility of natural processes. The third law indicates that as temperature approaches absolute zero, entropy approaches a constant minimum. Finally, the zeroth law defines thermal equilibrium, forming a foundation for temperature measurement. Thermodynamics has profound applications in engines, refrigerators, and the analysis of chemical reactions, impacting fields from engineering to climate science.'), 

ContentText(topic_title='Electromagnetism', content_text="Electromagnetism is a fundamental branch of physics that explores the interactions between electric and magnetic fields. Formulated by James Clerk Maxwell through his famous equations, it unites electricity and magnetism into a single theory. This theory explains a wide range of phenomena, from the repulsion between like-charged particles to the electromagnetic spectrum, which includes radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays. Electromagnetism is key to many technologies, including electric motors, generators, and transformers, as well as wireless communication systems. Beyond practical applications, the theory played a pivotal role in the development of modern physics, leading to relativistic electromagnetism which merges with Einstein’s theory of relativity to form a cornerstone of theoretical physics."),
